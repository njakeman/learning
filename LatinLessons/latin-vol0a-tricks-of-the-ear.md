# Latin Reference — Vol. 0.a: Tricks of the Ear

An interstitial at the foundations. Nothing in this volume is new — every form here lives in Vols I–X — but it is all *re-cut for the ear*, in the spirit of the Michel Thomas method: small sounds carrying big grammar, hooks you can hear before you can explain them. Grammar tables are for the eyes; this volume is for the train, the kettle, and the half-attentive drill. Read it aloud or not at all.

## The six people — endings as sounds you already own

The personal endings (Vol. IV) run the whole verb system. Each has an English hook, and two of them are not coincidences:

| Ending | Person | The hook |
|---|---|---|
| -ō / -m | I | **-m is Me** — amāba**m**, amē**m**, era**m**: hear m, think *me* |
| -s | you | English's own fossil: *thou goe**st**, ha**st**, do**st*** — the same ancient -s, still in the King James on your shelf. Hear -s, think *thou* |
| -t | he/she/it | **-t is iT** — ama**t**, fēci**t**, si**t** |
| -mus | we | **US is inside -mUS** — amā**mus**: *us*, doing it together |
| -tis | you (pl.) | **'tis ye** — the courtroom plural: amā**tis**, all of you |
| -nt | they | **-t plus N for maNy** — one letter turns *it* into *them*: ama**t** → ama**nt** |

The -s and -m hooks are genuine cousins, not mnemonics: English and Latin inherited the same Indo-European endings, so *thou lovest* and *amās* are the same machinery, weathered differently. You have been conjugating in the Latin manner since Sunday school.

## The four families — hear the vowel, know the tribe

The conjugations (Vol. IV) are vowel tribes. Say the infinitives aloud:

| Family | Sound | Members feel like |
|---|---|---|
| 1st: am**ā**re | the **AH** verbs | open-mouthed, regular, friendly |
| 2nd: mon**ē**re | the **EH** verbs | steady, well-behaved |
| 3rd: reg**e**re | **the mumbler** | short, unstressed, scruffy |
| 4th: aud**ī**re | the **EE** verbs | bright, tidy |

And the single most useful aural trick in the library — **stress tells 2nd from 3rd apart**. In -āre, -ēre, -īre verbs the stress sits *on the ending*: am**Ā**re, mon**Ē**re, aud**Ī**re. In the third conjugation it retreats to the stem: **RÉ**gere, **MÍT**tere, **CÁ**pere. You can hear which family a verb belongs to before you can spell it. Duolingo's audio, whatever its other sins, gets this right — listen for where the word leans.

## Tense signatures — four sounds that timestamp everything

| Hear | Think | Because |
|---|---|---|
| **-bā-** | *was / used to* | the imperfect's baa — the sound of the past bleating away: amā**bā**t (Vol. VII) |
| **bo-bi-bu** | *tomorrow* (AH and EH verbs only) | amā**bō**, amā**bi**s, amā**bu**nt (Vol. VIII) |
| **-v- + ī** | *I've* | amā**vī** — the v of *I've loved*: pure coincidence, permanently sticky (Vol. VIII) |
| **-erā-** | *had* | amāv**erā**mus — an **era** ago, already done (Vol. VIII) |

Add the mood-smell from Vol. X — **an unexpected ā is a wish** (*venit* → *veniat*) — and the ear can date and mood-check most verbs before the eye finishes reading them.

## The five constants — one rule where you feared five

The declensions (Vols I–II) look like five separate mountains. Underneath, five constants run through **all of them**, and they do most of the work of reading:

| Constant | The sound | Across the declensions |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Take aiM** | acc. singular ends **-m** | puella**m**, amīcu**m**, mīlite**m**, exercitu**m**, die**m** |
| **2. Long vowel + s** | acc. plural | puell**ās**, amīc**ōs**, mīlit**ēs**, exercit**ūs**, di**ēs** |
| **3. Of the crowd: -um** | gen. plural ends **-um** | puell**ārum**, amīc**ōrum**, mīlit**um**, exercit**uum**, di**ērum** |
| **4. Everyone on the bus** | dat./abl. plural = **-īs** or **-bus** | puell**īs**, amīc**īs**, mīlit**ibus**, exercit**ibus**, di**ēbus** — to, for, by and with them all: the whole crowd rides the same bus |
| **5. The neuter law** | nom = acc, and the plural is **-a** | templum/templ**a**, flūmen/flūmin**a** — always, without exception, forever (Vol. I) |

Learn these five as *sounds* and an unfamiliar noun in the wild is already half-parsed: hear -m, it's a target; hear -bus, someone's receiving or accompanying in bulk; hear final -a on a thing, suspect a neuter plural. The individual declension tables then refine what the constants have already roughed out — which is a far kinder order of operations than memorising five tables cold.

## Chants for the pocket

Three rhythms worth owning as pure sound, meaning optional at first:

> **-ō -s -t · -mus -tis -nt** — the drumbeat under every tense; say it as a beat, not a list.
> **sum es est · sumus estis sunt** — the same drum, played by the one verb that never behaves elsewhere.
> **hic haec hoc** — Vol. III.a's pointer, front-loaded; the rest of its table rhymes off these three.

The Wednesday train has a rhythm of its own; paradigms chanted to it stick better than paradigms read. (The dog walk stays chant-free, per the Charter — meditation by stealth is not to be conjugated at.)

## The drill

One hook per day, no more. The test comes free with Duolingo: when a form is *spoken*, name the person and tense **from sound alone, before translating** — "-bā- plus -t: he-was-doing-something" — and only then let the meaning land. When the timestamp and the person arrive before the vocabulary does, the ear is trained, and every volume above this one gets easier to hold.
