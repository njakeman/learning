# Latin Reference — Vol. VIII: The Time Machine

The volume where the verb system becomes complete. So far the shelf holds three tenses: the present (Vol. IV), the perfect in its third-person stone forms (Vol. V), and the imperfect (Vol. VII). Latin has six, and the remaining three — future, pluperfect, future perfect — come cheaply once you see the architecture, because the whole system is built from just **two stems** and the personal endings you already own.

First, a pleasing fact about the word *tense* itself: it has nothing to do with tension. It comes, via Old French *tens*, from **tempus** — time. Every tense is literally a *time*. This volume is therefore about tempus in both senses, and it ends with the largest vocabulary delivery in the library so far, because verbs are where Latin hides most of its English.

## The two-stem architecture

Every indicative tense is one of two stems, plus a signature, plus the endings from Vol. IV:

| Stem | Built on it | The logic |
|---|---|---|
| **Present stem** (amā-, mitt-) | present · imperfect · **future** | action viewed as ongoing or ahead |
| **Perfect stem** (amāv-, mīs-) | perfect · **pluperfect** · **future perfect** | action viewed as complete |

Three tenses per stem; you already know two of the first family and one of the second. The rest of this volume fills the three gaps.

## Principal parts — a verb's four-word biography

Dictionaries list Latin verbs as four forms, and the habit repays learning, because those four forms hand you every stem the verb will ever use:

> **mittō, mittere, mīsī, missum** — *send*

| Part | Form | What it gives you |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | mittō | *I send* — the present, and the conjugation's flavour |
| 2nd | mittere | the infinitive — confirms the conjugation (Vol. IV) |
| 3rd | mīsī | *I sent* — the **perfect stem** (mīs-), key to three tenses |
| 4th | missum | the "done" form — source of most English descendants |

The fourth part is the etymological jackpot: *missum* → mission, missile, transmit. English is largely built from Latin fourth principal parts, which is why learning them quadruple feels like extra work for about a week and then starts paying interest.

### How perfect stems are made — the five families

The perfect stem has to be learned per verb, but the verbs sort into families, and spotting the family halves the memory load:

| Family | Trick | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **-v-** | add v (the tidy majority of 1st and 4th conj.) | amāvī, audīvī |
| **-u-** | add u (most of 2nd conj.) | monuī, habuī |
| **-s-** | add s, with sound-collisions (g+s → x) | scrīpsī, dūxī, mīsī, mānsī |
| **Long vowel** | the stem vowel stretches, nothing else changes | vēnī, vīdī, ēgī, cēpī, fēcī |
| **Reduplication** | the verb *stutters* — its first sound doubles | dedī, stetī, cucurrī, momordī |

Reduplication is the fossil in the drawer: an ancient Indo-European way of marking the past, shared with Greek, surviving in Latin only in a handful of old, common verbs. *Cucurrī* — "I ran" — with the stem tripping over its own feet, which feels about right.

## The future — one tense, two machines

The awkward truth first: Latin builds the future two different ways depending on conjugation, and the split must simply be learned.

**1st and 2nd conjugations** take the signature **-bi-** (bō, bis, bit — the "bo-bi-bu" tense):

| Person | 1st: amābō | 2nd: monēbō |
|---|---|---|
| I | amābō | monēbō |
| you | amābis | monēbis |
| he/she/it | amābit | monēbit |
| we | amābimus | monēbimus |
| you (pl.) | amābitis | monēbitis |
| they | amābunt | monēbunt |

**3rd and 4th conjugations** instead shift vowel: **-am** for *I*, then **-ē-** throughout:

| Person | 3rd: regam | 3rd -iō: capiam | 4th: audiam |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | regam | capiam | audiam |
| you | regēs | capiēs | audiēs |
| he/she/it | reget | capiet | audiet |
| we | regēmus | capiēmus | audiēmus |
| you (pl.) | regētis | capiētis | audiētis |
| they | regent | capient | audient |

Two traps, both about lookalikes. First: *monēs* (you warn, present) against *regēs* (you will rule, future) — the ē means different things in different families, so the conjugation must be identified before the tense can be. The infinitive's vowel (Vol. IV) is the referee. Second, for much later: *regam* will turn out to share its shape with the subjunctive. A problem for a future volume — appropriately.

And **sum**, whose future is small and everywhere:

| | Sing. | Pl. |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | erō | erimus |
| 2nd | eris | eritis |
| 3rd | erit | erunt |

> **Aside — Ovid on friendship.** *Dōnec eris fēlīx, multōs numerābis amīcōs* — "as long as you are fortunate, you will count many friends" — written from exile on the Black Sea, where Ovid had discovered the second half of the couplet: cloudy times, and you'll be alone. Two futures (*eris*, *numerābis*), one of the most quoted bitter lines in Latin, and the tense doing the emotional work: friendship projected forward, conditionally.

## The perfect, completed

Vol. V gave the endings and the third-person stone forms; here is the full machine — perfect stem + its own unique set of endings (the only tense with endings all its own):

| Person | Ending | amāvī | vēnī |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | -ī | amāvī | vēnī |
| you | -istī | amāvistī | vēnistī |
| he/she/it | -it | amāvit | vēnit |
| we | -imus | amāvimus | vēnimus |
| you (pl.) | -istis | amāvistis | vēnistis |
| they | -ērunt | amāvērunt | vēnērunt |

English translates it two ways — *I loved* or *I have loved* — and Latin declines to distinguish; context carries it. Sum's perfect stem is **fu-**: fuī, fuistī, fuit — *I was / have been*.

> **Aside — the most famous perfects in the world.** After a five-day campaign in 47 BC, Caesar reported a victory to Rome in three words: **vēnī, vīdī, vīcī.**

<details>
<summary>Parse the boast — reveal</summary>

Three first-person perfects, all from the **long-vowel family** — veniō, videō, vincō with their stems stretched — and each just one syllable longer than its ending. *I came, I saw, I conquered.* No *et* between them (the rhetoricians call the missing conjunctions *asyndeton*): the connectives are omitted because the campaign didn't pause either. The grammar is the brag — three completed actions, filed as fact, in fewer letters than most people need for hello.

</details>

## The pluperfect — the past's own past

Perfect stem + what is transparently the imperfect of *sum* (Vol. VII's *eram*) bolted on. Meaning: *had done* — an action already complete before some other past moment.

| Person | Sing. | Pl. |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | amāveram | amāverāmus |
| 2nd | amāverās | amāverātis |
| 3rd | amāverat | amāverant |

> **Mīlitēs pontem fēcerant antequam hostēs vēnērunt.** — *The soldiers had built the bridge before the enemy came.* Two past events, and the pluperfect files them in order: bridge first, enemy second. (New words: **pōns, pontis** m. — bridge → pontoon, and the *pontifex*, Rome's chief priest, literally a bridge-builder; **hostis** — enemy → hostile; **antequam** — before.)

## The future perfect — the tense of law and curses

Perfect stem + *sum*'s future (erō, eris, erit… with third plural **-erint**). Meaning: *will have done* — complete at a point in the future.

| Person | Sing. | Pl. |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | amāverō | amāverimus |
| 2nd | amāveris | amāveritis |
| 3rd | amāverit | amāverint |

English barely uses its equivalent; Latin loved this tense, and nowhere more than in **law and curses** — the two genres that address hypothetical completed deeds. The Twelve Tables' archaic *quī malum carmen incantāssit* (Vol. VI.a) is essentially old dress for **incantāverit**: *whoever shall have chanted an evil spell*. The law doesn't punish intending; it punishes having done — and it has a tense that says precisely that.

### The Roman timestamp

The future perfect also exposes a habit of mind. Where English says *if you see the temple, you'll make an offering*, Latin timestamps properly:

> **Sī templum vīderis, dōnum dabis.** — *If you [will first have] seen the temple, you will give a gift.*

The seeing must be complete before the giving happens, so Latin puts it in the future perfect. English hides the sequence inside a vague present; Latin files events in their actual order, like a language designed by lawyers — which, on the evidence, it partly was.

### The showpiece

All of it together, in a sentence Silvianus would have recognised:

> **Quī ānulum meum cēperit, eum deae dōnābō.**

<details>
<summary>Reveal parse</summary>

*Whoever shall have taken my ring, I will give him to the goddess.* **Quī** — the curse's relative opener (Vols VI, VI.a). **Cēperit** — future perfect of capiō (long-vowel family): the theft treated as a completed future fact. **Eum** — *him*, Vol. III.a's pointer, accusative: the thief now the direct object of the giving. **Dōnābō** — first-conjugation future: the handover is a promise. One sentence, two new tenses, and the entire legal theology of Vol. VI: the crime, once complete, converts the criminal into the gift.

</details>

## The system entire

One verb, six tenses, third person, stone-compatible:

| Tense | Form | Meaning | Stem |
|---|---|---|---|
| Present | mittit | sends, is sending | present |
| Imperfect | mittēbat | was sending, used to send | present |
| Future | mittet | will send | present |
| Perfect | mīsit | sent, has sent | perfect |
| Pluperfect | mīserat | had sent | perfect |
| Future perfect | mīserit | will have sent | perfect |

That is the whole indicative, and there will never be a seventh. Everything later — subjunctives, infinitives of every tense, participles — is built from the same two stems, which is why the principal parts were worth the trouble.

## Worked sentences — the machine running

New vocabulary and new tenses together; mechanics named, per house custom:

| Latin | Translation | Mechanics |
|---|---|---|
| Crās nāvis ad portum veniet. | *Tomorrow the ship will come to the harbour.* | veniet: 4th-conj future · portus: 4th decl (Vol. II) · nāvis, nāvis f.: ship → navy, navigate |
| Herī epistulam scrīpsī; hodiē eam mittam. | *Yesterday I wrote a letter; today I'll send it.* | scrīpsī: s-family perfect · mittam: 3rd-conj future · eam: her/it, pointing at the letter (Vol. III.a) |
| In mānsiōne manēbimus. | *We shall stay at the inn.* | manēbimus: 2nd-conj future · **mānsiō, -ōnis** f.: a staying-place — the official rest-stops spaced along Roman roads, Britain's included; hence *mansion*, which has gone up in the world |
| Rōmānī in Britanniā diū mānsērunt. | *The Romans stayed in Britain a long time.* | mānsērunt: perfect, -ērunt = they · diū: adverb · about 367 years, since the tense invites the question |
| Semper tē amābō. | *I shall always love you.* | amābō: the bo-bi-bu future doing its softest work |
| Urbem vīderāmus antequam mūrōs fēcērunt. | *We had seen the city before they built the walls.* | vīderāmus: pluperfect ordering the past · urbs, urbis f.: city → urban |

## The drill

Three passes, train-sized. **One:** run *mittō* through all six tenses, third person, aloud — mittit, mittēbat, mittet, mīsit, mīserat, mīserit — until the two-stem seam is audible. **Two:** take any Vol. IV present-tense sentence and time-shift it through the whole system, six versions, one breath each. **Three:** the timestamp drill — build three *sī… (future perfect), … (future)* sentences of your own on the Silvianus pattern; if the logic of *vīderis before dabis* feels natural rather than fussy, the Roman habit of mind has taken.

> **Aside — the tense that worked.** Horace closed his third book of Odes, around 23 BC, with a boast built from this volume's two halves: **exēgī monumentum aere perennius** — *I have finished a monument more lasting than bronze* (perfect: done, filed) — and then, switching tense to reach forward: **nōn omnis moriar** — *I shall not wholly die.* A perfect and a future, wagering that the poems would outlast the empire's metalwork. The bronze is largely gone; you have just read the future tense in question, still running, two thousand and fifty years on. As advertisements for grammar go, it has the advantage of having been checked.
