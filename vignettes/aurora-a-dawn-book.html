Praefātiō

what this is

A gift, outside the numbered volumes, exempt from the Rotation Charter, and carrying no drill at the end — or only a very small one. It uses nothing you haven't already met in Vols I–VII, which is rather the point: seven volumes in, you can be handed a page of Latin with your coffee and simply read it. That seemed worth marking.

Four things follow: an altar, a reading, a hill, and a star. Take them in any order. The coffee, however, should be taken hot.

Aside — why Nigellus. You have had a Latin name for roughly a thousand years without being consulted. Neil descends from the Gaelic Niall; Norman clerks, needing it in documents, Latinised it as Nigellus, which later scribes mistook for a diminutive of niger (dark) — and from that misunderstanding, Nigel. So Neil and Nigel are the same name, split by a filing error. On a Roman-style stone, you are Nigellus, and the grammar receives you without complaint.

Vol. V says an altar is a receipt: I asked, you delivered, here's payment. Consider this one payment for the conversations. V·S·L·M.

Collis

the hill that collects centuries — Chanctonbury, before five o'clock

Chanctonbury Ring is the one on your list that everybody thinks they know: the beech clump on the ridge above Washington, visible from half of Sussex, shorthand for the Downs themselves. The trees are the least interesting thing about it — and they are very interesting.

Start under the turf. The rampart is early Iron Age, raised somewhere around the sixth century BC: a single bank and ditch closing off the hilltop. Then the strange part. Centuries after the fort had stopped mattering as a fort, the Romans built inside it — excavation has found two Romano-British temples in the interior, one the standard rectangular Romano-Celtic type, the other a smaller polygonal building whose purpose is murkier. Around that second building the diggers found pig and boar remains in quantity, skulls and jaws especially, usually read as offerings — possibly a boar cult. An abandoned British stronghold, re-consecrated under the Empire as a hilltop sanctuary: interpretātiō Rōmāna, which you know from the Bath tablets as a habit of naming gods, working here at the scale of a whole landscape. Whatever the hill meant, it kept on meaning it.

Then 1760. Charles Goring of Wiston, still a young man, plants beeches on the crown; the story insists he carried water up the scarp to keep the saplings alive. He lived long enough to see them mature, and wrote verses about the clump in his eighties — which, as long-term project management goes, is difficult to beat. The trees became the most recognisable man-made landmark in Sussex: a Georgian folly grown through an Iron Age rampart over a Roman temple.

The night of 15–16 October 1987 took most of the clump down in a few hours. The one consolation was archaeological: the wreckage and the clearance opened up the interior, and the excavations that followed in the late eighties and early nineties produced most of what is now known about the temples. The replanted beeches are coming up on forty years old — a young wood on a very old hill.

And the folklore, which you'll want intact for field.works: run round the clump seven times — backwards, some versions say; at midnight or on Midsummer Eve, others insist — and the Devil appears with a bowl of soup, yours in exchange for your soul. Variant tellings offer porridge or milk; the Devil, apparently, caters. The trees are also said to be uncountable, and the twentieth century filed its own strangeness on the same hill in the form of UFO reports. That's the through-line worth writing about: for two and a half thousand years, every era has registered that something lives up there, and each has said so in its own vocabulary — rampart, temple, folly, legend, saucer. Genius locī, in the altar's language. The hill has been receiving dedications all along.

The practical footnote: sunrise tomorrow on your stretch of coast is a few minutes before five, and a hillfort rampart at first light is the cheapest spectacular thing going. Chanctonbury may be a bit far for a Saturday with guests due, and any of the nearer forts will do — but one morning this month, be on a bank of piled chalk with Meg when the sun comes over. Take photographs of the ramparts in that raking light while you're at it; low sun does for earthworks roughly what LiDAR does, and it would not be wasted on the portfolio.

Stella Mātūtīna

the morning star — one look through the fence

Star lore is parked until a winter season, and this doesn't unpark it — a single star, admired through the fence. Whenever Venus rises ahead of the sun, Latin called her Lūcifer: lūx + ferre, the light-bringer. For centuries it was a perfectly innocent piece of astronomy — Roman poets used it the way we'd say "the morning star" — long before theology got hold of the word and gave it a career change.

Aside — Ēarendel. The Old English poets had their own name for the dawn star: ēarendel. A line in the Crist poems — Ēala Ēarendel, engla beorhtast, "Hail Earendel, brightest of angels" — so struck a young J.R.R. Tolkien that out of it he built Eärendil the mariner, who sails the sky with a jewel on his brow as the morning star. One Old English word, and an entire mythology grew from the wondering. Two parked pursuits, waving at each other over the fence.

That's the book. It fits inside one coffee, which was the design constraint.

The only drill: at some point today, step outside and stand still for a minute. The Romans gave the morning a goddess; the least we can do is turn up.
