# Familia Britannica — A Romano-British Reader

*Capitula I–II, with the map of the whole.*

## Praefātiō (the only English in the book)

This is the second parked capstone: a continuous story told entirely in Latin, in the manner of Hans Ørberg's *Familia Rōmāna* — the reader that teaches by never translating. The rules of the road:

The story uses only grammar already on the shelf, chapter by chapter, and each chapter's **Grammatica** section links back to the volumes that cover what it used. New words are gathered in **Glōssae** reveal-blocks after each scene — try the scene cold first; most meaning is guessable from context, cognates, and repetition, which is the method working as designed. Read everything aloud, twice. The **Pēnsum** (assignment) at each chapter's end asks questions in Latin and wants answers in Latin, however short.

Setting: **Noviomagus Rēgīnōrum** — Chichester — around AD 200, under the Downs. The household's names are of the kinds the British corpus actually yields: a Roman-named father, a British-named mother, and the mixed generation after them. The weasel is period-correct — households kept them for pest control before cats fully took over the posting — and readers of the reference shelf will recognise her as the library's presiding spirit.

Links assume this file sits at the repo root; if it lives in a folder, prefix the paths with `../`.

## Capitulum I — Familia in Britanniā

Britannia īnsula est. Britannia īnsula magna est. Circum Britanniam Ōceanus est. In Ōceanō multae īnsulae sunt, sed Britannia magna īnsula est.

In Britanniā multa oppida sunt. Londinium oppidum magnum est. Noviomagus oppidum parvum est. Londinium et Noviomagus oppida Rōmāna sunt. Noviomagus prope mare est. In Britanniā etiam viae sunt. Viae Rōmānae longae et rēctae sunt.

| Vocābulum | Adnotātiō |
|---|---|
| īnsula | land with water all round — Britain is one |
| Ōceanus | the Ocean |
| oppidum | a town |
| prope (+ acc.) | near |
| circum (+ acc.) | around |
| mare | the sea |
| rēctus, -a, -um | straight (regō's family — Vol. VIII) |

</details>

Prope Noviomagum casa est. In casā familia habitat. Candidus pater est. Senovara māter est. Fēlīx fīlius est. Lūcia fīlia est. Candidus mercātor Rōmānus est. Senovara fēmina Britannica est. Fēlīx et Lūcia līberī sunt.

Ecce! In casā etiam mustela est! Mustela nōn magna sed parva est. Nōmen mustelae Fūr est. Cūr "Fūr"? Quod mustela omnia captat! Fūr ānulōs captat, nummōs captat, etiam cāseum captat.

Fēlīx et Lūcia mustelam amant. Senovara mustelam nōn amat. "Mustela mala est!" inquit Senovara. Sed Candidus rīdet.

Estne mustela mala? Fortasse. Sed familia mustelam habet, et mustela familiam habet.

| Vocābulum | Adnotātiō |
|---|---|

### Pēnsum I

Respondē Latīnē: Ubi est Noviomagus? Quis est māter? Estne Candidus mercātor an mīles? Cūr nōmen mustelae "Fūr" est? Quis mustelam nōn amat?

### Grammatica I

Everything here is early-shelf: *est/sunt* and the third person ([Vol. IV](LatinLessons/latin-vol4-verbs.md)); nominatives naming and genitive possession, with *in* + ablative for place and *prope/circum* + accusative ([Vol. I](LatinLessons/latin-vol1-cases.md)); adjectives agreeing across the board — *multa oppida, viae longae* ([Vol. III](LatinLessons/latin-vol3-adjectives.md)); and the small words *-ne, quod, etiam, sed* ([Vol. III.a](LatinLessons/latin-vol3a-demonstratives.md)).

## Capitulum II — Ānulus abest!

Candidus ānulum aureum habēbat. Ānulus pulcher erat, et Candidus eum amābat. Herī ānulus in mēnsā erat. Hodiē ānulus abest!

"Ubi est ānulus meus?" clāmat Candidus. "Quis ānulum cēpit?"

"Ego ānulum nōn cēpī!" inquit Fēlīx.

"Ego ānulum nōn vīdī!" inquit Lūcia.

"Ego ānulum nōn cēpī," inquit Senovara. "Sed ubi est mustela?"

<details>
<summary>Glōssae</summary>

| Vocābulum | Adnotātiō |
|---|---|
| ānulus | a ring (Lectiō II's word, spelled properly) |
| aureus, -a, -um | golden (aurum = gold) |
| abest | is away, is missing (ab + est) |
| herī / hodiē | yesterday / today (Vol. VIII's time words) |
| mēnsa | a table |
| clāmat | shouts |
| cēpit / cēpī | took / I took (Vol. VIII: capiō's long-vowel perfect) |

</details>

Familia mustelam quaerit. Fēlīx sub mēnsā quaerit. Lūcia post lectum quaerit. Senovara in culīnā quaerit. Nēmō mustelam videt.

Tandem Candidus parvum forāmen in mūrō videt. In forāmine sedet Fūr! Et in forāmine sunt: ānulus aureus, trēs nummī, ūna fībula, et magnum frustum cāseī.

"Ō Fūr!" clāmat Candidus. "Tū ānulum meum cēpistī! Tū vērō fūr es!"

Senovara rīdet. "Nunc tabulam scrībe, Candide: QVĪ ĀNVLVM MEVM CĒPERIT…"

Sed Candidus mustelam nōn pūnit. Cūr? Quod Fūr mūrēs quoque captat, et mustela ūtilis est. Et Candidus mustelam amat — sed id nōn dīcit.

<details>
<summary>Glōssae</summary>

| Vocābulum | Adnotātiō |
|---|---|
| quaerit | looks for, searches |
| sub (+ abl.) | under (location — Vol. I's in/sub rule) |
| lectus | a bed, a couch |
| culīna | kitchen (→ culinary) |
| nēmō | nobody |
| tandem | at last |
| forāmen | a hole |
| mūrus | a wall |
| fībula | a brooch — the safety-pin of the ancient world, and the commonest small find on any Romano-British site |
| frustum | a piece, a lump |
| tabula | a tablet — Senovara is proposing a dēfīxiō (Vol. VI), and quoting Vol. VIII's future perfect to do it |
| pūnit | punishes |
| mūs, mūrēs | mouse, mice (Vol. VII's other party) |
| ūtilis | useful (a third-declension adjective — Vol. III) |

</details>

### Pēnsum II

Respondē Latīnē: Quid Candidus habēbat? Ubi erat ānulus herī? Quis ānulum cēpit? Quid in forāmine erat? Cūr Candidus mustelam nōn pūnit? Amatne Candidus mustelam?

### Grammatica II

The chapter runs on the past system: imperfect for background (*habēbat, erat, amābat* — [Vol. VII](LatinLessons/latin-vol7-verb-workshop.md)) against perfect for events (*cēpit, cēpistī, cēpī* across three persons — [Vol. VIII](LatinLessons/latin-vol8-time-machine.md)); *sub* + ablative for where things are looked for ([Vol. I](LatinLessons/latin-vol1-cases.md)); a vocative in the wild (*Candide!* — Vol. I's rarest case, deployed by a wife); and one quoted future perfect, capitals and all, for readers of [Vol. VI](LatinLessons/latin-vol6-curse-tablets.md) and Lectiō II to enjoy. The closing line — *sed id nōn dīcit* — is a small door left open for [Vol. IX](LatinLessons/latin-vol9-reported-world.md).
