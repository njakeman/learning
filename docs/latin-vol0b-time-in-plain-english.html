Latin Reference — Vol. 0.b: Time in Plain English

A foundations volume with a single promise: by the end of it, pluperfect will feel obvious. The fog around tense names has a specific cause — the names are themselves untranslated Latin, handed down by Roman grammarians and never converted into English. Meanwhile you already use every one of these six tenses fluently, dozens of times a day, without knowing what they're called. Nothing here is new grammar; this volume just pins name tags on things you do at the kitchen table.

Decoding the names

Translate the labels and half the mystery evaporates:

Grammar name The Latin behind it Meaning in plain English
Present praesēns "at hand" — happening now
Imperfect imperfectum "unfinished" — was still going on
Future futūrum "about to be" (Vol. IX's participle, naming a tense)
Perfect perfectum "done" (per + facere, "carried through") — nothing to do with flawless
Pluperfect plūs quam perfectum "more than done" — done before something else that's also done
Future perfect futūrum perfectum "will-be-done"

So perfect means finished, imperfect means unfinished, and pluperfect is a squashed-down "more-than-done". The terminology was descriptive all along; it just never got subtitled.

The two questions every tense answers

Here is the whole system as one idea. Every tense answers exactly two questions:

  1. From which vantage point are we looking? Past, now, or future.
  2. Is the action rolling, or wrapped up? Ongoing footage, or a done deal.

Three vantage points × two states = six tenses. That's the entire inventory:

Viewed from the past Viewed from now Viewed from the future
Ongoing was writing — imperfect writes / is writing — present will write — future
Done had written — pluperfect wrote / has written — perfect will have written — future perfect

Two things fall out of this grid. First, the three "done" tenses are one idea launched from three vantage points: done before then (pluperfect), done as of now (perfect), done by then (future perfect). Second — and this is why Vol. VIII's architecture looks the way it does — Latin builds the top row on the present stem and the bottom row on the perfect stem. The two stems aren't an arbitrary filing system; they are the grid's two rows. One stem per answer to question 2.

Each tense, grounded

Imperfect — the background camera

English forms: was writing, used to write, and — the sneaky one — habitual would: "every summer we would swim in the river." All imperfect. This is the tense of scene-setting and rolling footage: the camera pans across what was going on. "The rain was falling, the kettle was boiling, the dog was asleep on the sofa" — three imperfects painting a scene, no events yet. The name says it exactly: unfinished — at the moment we're looking at, the action hadn't wrapped.

Perfect — the snapshot

English forms: wrote and has written — and here is the single biggest friction point between the languages, so it gets its own flag: English has two past forms where Latin has one. "She wrote" and "she has written" are both scrīpsit; context supplies the flavour. English also wraps its questions and negatives in did — "did you see it?", "I didn't go" — and all of that did-machinery is, in Latin, just the perfect: vīdistī? If the imperfect is footage, the perfect is the click of a shutter: the event, complete, filed. In storytelling the division of labour is clean — imperfect paints the scene, perfect advances the plot: "The rain was falling (imperfect — scene)… and then the phone rang (perfect — plot)."

Pluperfect — the flashback

The "had" tense, and once you hear that, the opacity is gone. You use it whenever a story needs to step behind its own timeline: "When I got to the platform (perfect), the train had left (pluperfect)." "She realised (perfect) she had forgotten the keys (pluperfect)." Both verbs are past, but one is further past — done before the other done thing. That's all "more than done" ever meant.

And notice the pairing is structural, not accidental: a pluperfect rarely travels alone, because "more than done" needs a "done" to be more than. The perfect is the anchor — it plants the reference point in the past — and the pluperfect steps behind it. Even a lone "the train had left" smuggles its anchor in: the arriving is implied. So when you meet a pluperfect, on the page or in your own mouth, look for its perfect partner; it is almost always in the same sentence or the one before. Every flashback in every anecdote you've ever told ran on this pairing; you were fluent in the pluperfect long before you'd heard of it.

Future perfect — the deadline

The "will have" tense: looking back from a point that hasn't arrived yet. "By Friday I will have finished the report (future perfect)." "By the time you read this, I'll have landed" — 'll have landed future perfect, and read worth a second glance: present in form, future in meaning. English quietly uses the present tense in by-the-time clauses about the future; Latin refuses the fudge and timestamps it future perfect (lēgeris — Vol. VIII's Roman timestamp, vindicated by your own sentence patterns). English uses this tense more than people think — every "by [deadline], done" sentence is one. Latin, as Vol. VIII showed, positively loved it, because law and curses both address deeds that will-be-done: quī cēperit — whoever will-have-taken.

Spot the tense

Everyday English; name the tense before revealing. Each reveal gives the reasoning and the Latin form it maps to (verbs from Vol. VIII's bank).

1. "I was reading when you rang."

Reveal

Was reading — imperfect (background footage): legēbam. Rang — perfect (the event that interrupts): the classic scene-then-plot pairing in one sentence.

2. "Every winter they would walk the old drove road."

Reveal

Imperfect — habitual would is past routine, not a conditional: ambulābant. (When would means "if only / in that case", it belongs to Vol. X's subjunctive instead — English overloads one little word with both jobs.)

3. "By the time we arrived, the film had started."

Reveal

Two tenses here, working as a pair. Arrived — perfect: the anchor event, plain past, done. Had started — pluperfect: done before the arriving — "more than done", measured against the anchor. In Latin: Ubi vēnimus, fābula iam coeperat — vēnimus perfect, coeperat pluperfect, with iam ("already") supplying the ruefulness. The flashback tense, in its natural habitat of mild disappointment.

4. "Did you see the stone by the gate?"

Reveal

Perfect — English's did-wrapper dissolves entirely in Latin: vīdistī? One word, question mark included by tone.

5. "By September I'll have saved enough."

Reveal

Future perfect — the deadline tense: servāverō-shaped ("will have saved"). Looking back over the shoulder from a point ahead.

6. "She has climbed that hill a hundred times."

Reveal

Perfect — has climbed and climbed are the same Latin form: ascendit (perfect). The English has/­-ed split is a distinction Latin simply declines to make.

The whole grid in one paragraph

One last pass — every tense in a single homely story. Read it, label each verb, then check:

The train was crawling through the cutting. I had forgotten my book, so I stared out of the window instead. These days I keep a spare in the bag. No doubt I'll forget something else tomorrow — and by Friday I'll have forgotten what it was.

Reveal

was crawling — imperfect (scene) · had forgotten — pluperfect (the step further back) · stared — perfect (the plot, such as it is) · keep — present · 'll forget — future · 'll have forgotten — future perfect (done by Friday's vantage point). Six tenses, one commute, no grammar required to produce it — only to name it.

The drill

Two habits, no desk needed. One: for a day, catch yourself mid-sentence whenever you say had — "the train had left", "I had meant to" — and silently tag it pluperfect. Hearing the tense in your own mouth demystifies it faster than any table. Two: the grid drill — pick a verb, fill all six English cells aloud (was writing, writes, will write; had written, wrote, will have written), then swap in the Latin from Vol. VIII's system table. When the two grids feel like the same grid — because they are — this volume has done its job and the names will never fog again.
