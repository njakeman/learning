Latin Reference — Vol. 0.a: Tricks of the Ear

An interstitial at the foundations. Nothing in this volume is new — every form here lives in Vols I–X — but it is all re-cut for the ear, in the spirit of the Michel Thomas method: small sounds carrying big grammar, hooks you can hear before you can explain them. Grammar tables are for the eyes; this volume is for the train, the kettle, and the half-attentive drill. Read it aloud or not at all. One verb carries most of the demonstrations below — amō, amāre, to love, the first conjugation's traditional workhorse — and every Latin form is glossed where it appears, because a hook you can't verify isn't a hook.

The six people — endings as sounds you already own

The personal endings (Vol. IV) run the whole verb system. Each has an English hook, and two of them are not coincidences:

Ending Person The hook
-ō / -m I -m is Me — amābam (I was loving), eram (I was): hear m, think me
-s you English's own fossil: thou goest, hast, dost — the same ancient -s, still in the King James on your shelf. Hear -s, think thou
-t he/she/it -t is iT — amat (he loves), fēcit (he made — Vol. V's stone verb), sit (may it be — the verb inside R.I.P., Vol. X)
-mus we US is inside -mUS — amāmus (we love): us, doing it together
-tis you (pl.) 'tis ye — the courtroom plural: amātis (you all love)
-nt they -t plus N for maNy — one letter turns it into them: amat → amant (he loves → they love)

The -s and -m hooks are genuine cousins, not mnemonics: English and Latin inherited the same Indo-European endings, so thou lovest and amās are the same machinery, weathered differently. You have been conjugating in the Latin manner since Sunday school.

The four families — hear the vowel, know the tribe

The conjugations (Vol. IV) are vowel tribes. Say the infinitives aloud:

Family Sound Members feel like
1st: amāre the AH verbs open-mouthed, regular, friendly
2nd: monēre the EH verbs steady, well-behaved
3rd: regere the mumbler short, unstressed, scruffy
4th: audīre the EE verbs bright, tidy

And the single most useful aural trick in the library — stress tells 2nd from 3rd apart. In -āre, -ēre, -īre verbs the stress sits on the ending: amĀre, monĒre, audĪre. In the third conjugation it retreats to the stem: RÉgere, MÍTtere, CÁpere. You can hear which family a verb belongs to before you can spell it. Duolingo's audio, whatever its other sins, gets this right — listen for where the word leans.

Tense signatures — four sounds that timestamp everything

Hear Think Because
-bā- was / used to the imperfect's baa — the sound of the past bleating away: amābat (he was loving, Vol. VII)
bo-bi-bu tomorrow (AH and EH verbs only) amābō (I shall love), amābis (you will love), amābunt (they will love) (Vol. VIII)
-v- + ī I've amāvī (I've loved / I loved) — the v of I've: pure coincidence, permanently sticky (Vol. VIII)
-erā- had amāverāmus (we had loved) — an era ago, already done (Vol. VIII)

Add the mood-smell from Vol. X — an unexpected ā is a wish (venit → veniat: he comes → may he come) — and the ear can date and mood-check most verbs before the eye finishes reading them.

The five constants — one rule where you feared five

The declensions (Vols I–II) look like five separate mountains. Underneath, five constants run through all of them, and they do most of the work of reading. The parade nouns below are the library's own, Vols I–II: puella girl, amīcus friend, mīles soldier, exercitus army, diēs day — with templum temple and flūmen river standing in for the neuter law:

Constant The sound Across the declensions
1. Take aiM acc. singular ends -m puellam, amīcum, mīlitem, exercitum, diem
2. Long vowel + s acc. plural puellās, amīcōs, mīlitēs, exercitūs, diēs
3. Of the crowd: -um gen. plural ends -um puellārum, amīcōrum, mīlitum, exercituum, diērum
4. Everyone on the bus dat./abl. plural = -īs or -bus puellīs, amīcīs, mīlitibus, exercitibus, diēbus — to, for, by and with them all: the whole crowd rides the same bus
5. The neuter law nom = acc, and the plural is -a templum/templa, flūmen/flūmina — always, without exception, forever (Vol. I)

Learn these five as sounds and an unfamiliar noun in the wild is already half-parsed: hear -m, it's a target; hear -bus, someone's receiving or accompanying in bulk; hear final -a on a thing, suspect a neuter plural. The individual declension tables then refine what the constants have already roughed out — which is a far kinder order of operations than memorising five tables cold.

Chants for the pocket

Three rhythms worth owning as pure sound, meaning optional at first:

-ō -s -t · -mus -tis -nt — the drumbeat under every tense; say it as a beat, not a list. sum es est · sumus estis sunt — the same drum, played by the one verb that never behaves elsewhere. hic haec hoc — Vol. III.a's pointer, front-loaded; the rest of its table rhymes off these three.

The Wednesday train has a rhythm of its own; paradigms chanted to it stick better than paradigms read. (The dog walk stays chant-free, per the Charter — meditation by stealth is not to be conjugated at.)

The drill

One hook per day, no more. The test comes free with Duolingo: when a form is spoken, name the person and tense from sound alone, before translating — "-bā- plus -t: he-was-doing-something" — and only then let the meaning land. When the timestamp and the person arrive before the vocabulary does, the ear is trained, and every volume above this one gets easier to hold.
